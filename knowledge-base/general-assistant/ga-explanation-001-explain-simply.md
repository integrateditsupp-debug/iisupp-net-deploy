# Explaining technology in simple language

## 1. Principle
Find the analogy first, then introduce one technical term at a time, only if needed.

## 2. Common explanations

**The internet:** "A web of computers all connected by wires. Your laptop talks to other computers somewhere else, and back."

**A web browser:** "Your window into the internet. You type where you want to go, it shows you the page."

**A website:** "Like a magazine or a store, but on a computer. Each page has its own address."

**An email:** "A letter, but it gets there in seconds and you don't pay for stamps."

**A password:** "A secret word that proves it's you. Don't share it; don't reuse the same one everywhere."

**Two-factor / MFA:** "An extra check after the password — like a second key. Even if someone steals your password, they can't get in without your phone."

**The cloud:** "Storage on someone else's computer that you can reach from anywhere. Your photos on iCloud are an example."

**A virus / malware:** "A program someone made to do harm — steal data or break things. Antivirus software watches for them."

**Phishing:** "A trick email pretending to be from someone you trust, hoping you give up a password or money."

**Wi-Fi:** "Internet without a cable — broadcast as radio waves between your device and a router (the box plugged in somewhere in your house or office)."

**A router:** "The traffic controller for your internet. Your devices connect to it; it talks to the internet."

**An app:** "A program designed for one job. Photos, banking, email — each is its own app."

**Streaming (Netflix, Spotify):** "Watching or listening live from the internet, without storing the whole thing on your device."

## 3. ARIA's pattern
- Hear the user's question.
- Pick the simplest analogy that matches.
- Wait for understanding.
- If they want more, go one level deeper.
- Never pile up jargon.

## 4. Test
- "Did that make sense?"
- "Want me to explain differently?"
- "Want a real-life example?"
